Historical and Legal Basis of IP Rights in the Philippines

Understanding the roots and legal protection of Indigenous Peoples

Indios (Christianized Lowlanders)

Indios was the colonial term used by the Spanish to classify the indigenous, Christianized populations of the Philippine lowlands who were integrated into the colonial administrative and religious system. Unlike the Moros of the south or the upland tribal groups, Indios were the primary subjects of the reduccion policy, which resettled scattered communities into centralized towns organized around a church plaza. Over centuries of Spanish rule, these groups—including the Tagalog, Visayan, Ilocano, and Bicolano—developed a unique identity that fused native Austronesian customs with Roman Catholic traditions and Hispanic social structures. While originally a label imposed by colonizers to denote social inferiority, the term was eventually subverted by 19th-century Filipino intellectuals, or Ilustrados, who reclaimed the identity to fuel the nationalist movement that led to the Philippine Revolution and the birth of a unified Filipino nation.

Moros (Muslim Communities in the South)

The Moro people, or Bangsamoro, are a collective of 13 indigenous ethnolinguistic groups in the southern Philippines—most notably the Maguindanao, Maranao, and Tausug—who have preserved a distinct Islamic identity for centuries. Originally a colonial label used by the Spanish, the term "Moro" was reclaimed by these communities as a symbol of pride and unified resistance against foreign colonization and for the protection of their ancestral homelands in Mindanao, the Sulu Archipelago, and Palawan. Today, this identity is formally recognized through the establishment of the Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM), representing a rich cultural mosaic that blends Sunni Islamic traditions with indigenous Austronesian heritage while striving for lasting peace and self-determination within the Philippine nation.

Infieles (Upland Indigenous Communities)

Infieles (meaning "infidels" or "unbelievers") was the collective term used by Spanish colonial authorities to describe the diverse indigenous communities inhabiting the upland and interior regions of the Philippines who remained outside the reach of Christian conversion and colonial administration. Primarily consisting of groups such as the Cordilleran peoples (Igorots) of the north and various Lumad tribes in Mindanao, these communities were defined by their fierce independence and preservation of ancestral, animist traditions. Unlike the lowland Indios or the Muslim Moros, the Infieles maintained their pre-colonial social structures and warrior cultures by utilizing the rugged mountain terrain as a natural defense against foreign subjugation. Today, these groups are celebrated as the guardians of the Philippines' most authentic indigenous heritage, representing a legacy of cultural resistance that refused to be assimilated by centuries of colonial rule.

1987 Philippine Constitution

Article II, Section 22 mandates the State to recognize and promote the rights of Indigenous Cultural Communities, protecting ancestral lands and cultural traditions.

Indigenous Peoples’ Rights Act of 1997 (RA 8371)

IPRA recognizes ICCs/IPs as a distinct sector and guarantees rights to ancestral domains, self-governance, social justice, and cultural integrity.

UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007)

UNDRIP establishes international standards recognizing Indigenous Peoples’ rights to self-determination, land ownership, and Free, Prior, and Informed Consent (FPIC).
