Indigenous Peoples' Rights & Laws

Indigenous Peoples’ Rights Act (RA 8371)

Enacted in 1997, RA 8371 recognizes Indigenous Cultural Communities/Indigenous Peoples (ICCs/IPs) as a distinct sector. It ensures protection of their ancestral lands, governance, culture, and basic rights. The law empowers IPs to participate in policy-making and safeguards them from displacement and exploitation.

Indigenous Peoples have the right to own, occupy, develop, and manage their ancestral lands and territories. This includes forests, rivers, and natural resources. It protects them from forced eviction and guarantees security of land tenure. Learn more about Ancestral Domain Rights.

ICCs/IPs can maintain their traditional political structures, decision-making processes, and leadership systems. They are empowered to manage internal affairs, resolve conflicts, and implement community rules in accordance with their customs. Further reading.

Protects indigenous peoples from discrimination and marginalization. Ensures access to social services such as education, healthcare, and livelihood support. Provides legal recourse for rights violations. More on Human Rights for IPs.

Safeguards languages, traditions, beliefs, and rituals. Encourages the preservation of cultural heritage, arts, music, and ceremonies, passing these to future generations. Read more.

Other Relevant Laws Protecting Indigenous Peoples

Protects children, including indigenous children, from abuse, exploitation, and discrimination. Ensures their well-being and growth in a safe and nurturing environment. Official text.

Aims to protect lands, rivers, and forests in indigenous territories. Promotes sustainable practices to conserve natural resources and reduce pollution. Full law.

Ensures culturally appropriate education programs that respond to the needs of indigenous communities. Encourages mother-tongue instruction and preservation of cultural knowledge. Learn more.

No project or activity may proceed on ancestral lands without the free, prior, and informed consent of the community. Ensures IPs have full understanding of projects affecting their lands and the power to approve or reject them. More about FPIC.
